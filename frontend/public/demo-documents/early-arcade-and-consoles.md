# The birth of video games: arcade and home consoles

Video games began in research labs and arcades before finding a place in the living room. The first commercial arcade game, **Computer Space** (1971), was soon followed by **Pong** (1972), which turned pinball parlors into video game venues. Atari and other manufacturers released dedicated home consoles that played a single game or a small set of games built into the hardware.

The **Atari 2600** (1977) popularized the cartridge model: one console, many swappable game cartridges. This made it possible to build a library of games and turned the console into a platform. Titles like *Space Invaders*, *Pac-Man*, and *Pitfall!* became household names. The crash of 1983, driven by market saturation and poor-quality titles, shook the industry in North America but did not stop its growth in Japan, where the **Famicom** (Nintendo Entertainment System) laid the groundwork for the next decade.

## Key ideas from the early era

**Arcade culture:** Coin-operated cabinets were social spaces. High scores and leaderboards encouraged competition and repeat play. Design focused on short sessions and increasing difficulty to keep players inserting coins.

**Home consoles:** Cartridges allowed third-party developers to publish without building hardware. This led to both classic hits and a flood of low-quality games, contributing to the 1983 crash. Recovery came with stricter quality control and beloved franchises that defined the next generation.
