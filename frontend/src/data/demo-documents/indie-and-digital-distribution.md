# Indie games and digital distribution

From the mid-2000s onward, digital storefronts and accessible tools made it possible for small teams and solo developers to release games without a publisher. **Steam**, **Xbox Live Arcade**, **PlayStation Network**, and later **itch.io** and the **Nintendo eShop** gave indies a direct path to players. Lower barriers to entry led to a surge of creative and experimental titles that often explored ideas too niche or risky for big-budget studios.

**Braid**, **Limbo**, **Super Meat Boy**, and **Fez** helped establish “indie” as a recognizable category. **Minecraft** started as an indie project before becoming one of the best-selling games of all time. Roguelikes, narrative adventures, and pixel-art platformers flourished. Crowdfunding (Kickstarter, Fig) and engines like **Unity** and **Unreal** (with more approachable licensing) further enabled small teams to fund and build ambitious projects.

## What changed

**Discovery and curation:** With thousands of releases each year, storefronts had to solve discovery. Tags, recommendations, and featuring by platform holders became crucial. Events like the **Indie Megabooth** and **Day of the Devs** gave indies visibility at conventions.

**Diversity of design:** Indie games often focused on a single mechanic, a strong narrative, or a distinct art style. They proved that commercial success did not require huge budgets, and they influenced both other indies and larger studios, reinforcing the idea that games could be personal, experimental, and still find an audience.
