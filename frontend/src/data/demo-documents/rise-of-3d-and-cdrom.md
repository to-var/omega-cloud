# The rise of 3D and CD-ROM

The 1990s saw video games move from 2D sprites to 3D worlds and from cartridges to optical discs. The **Sega Saturn**, **Sony PlayStation**, and **Nintendo 64** competed for dominance. CD-ROM offered far more storage than cartridges, enabling full-motion video, orchestral soundtracks, and large amounts of voice and texture data. Cartridges remained faster to load and allowed for custom chips (as in some N64 games), but the cost and capacity of CDs made them the standard for the following generation.

**3D graphics** changed both gameplay and storytelling. Games like *Super Mario 64* and *The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time* set the template for 3D platforming and action-adventure. First-person shooters such as *Doom* and *Quake* defined PC gaming and online multiplayer. Real-time 3D required new tools: level editors, 3D modeling, and engines that could handle polygons, lighting, and camera systems.

## Lasting impact

**Storage and media:** CD-ROM (and later DVD and Blu-ray) made large-scale content feasible. Cutscenes, voice acting, and open worlds became expected in many genres. The shift to digital distribution in the 2000s continued this trend without physical media limits.

**3D as default:** After the mid-1990s, most major releases were 3D. 2D remained important in handhelds, indie games, and stylized titles, but 3D became the norm for blockbuster and flagship experiences.
